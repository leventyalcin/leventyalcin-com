Where we sit, relative to every modern top.

Each line is the S&P 500 anchored to its pre-crash high (month 0 = the peak). It shows the year of run-up into the top and the crash that followed. Today the index is at a fresh record, so the white line ends right at the peak: the run-up is visible, the aftermath is not yet written.

HOW TO READ THIS
Lines are normalised, not absolute: a value of 100 is each cycle's own peak, 80 means 20% below that peak. The x-axis is months before/after the top, so the four eras line up on the same moment of maximum optimism. The current run-up (+27.7% over the trailing year) is the steepest of the four into a peak, and valuations are rich (forward P/E above 22 vs a ~19 average). That is a real echo of 1999. But a steep run-up is not a timing signal: markets sat near highs for months before 2000 and 2007 turned, and 2020's crash had nothing to do with valuation at all. Past chart shapes do not predict the next path, and "we are at a peak" is only ever knowable in hindsight. This is an illustration for context, not investment advice.
