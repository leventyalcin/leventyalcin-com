HOW TO READ THIS
Lines are normalised, not absolute: a value of 100 is each cycle's own peak, 80 means 20% below that peak. The x-axis is months before/after the top, so the four eras line up on the same moment of maximum optimism. The current run-up (+27.7% over the trailing year) is the steepest of the four into a peak, and valuations are rich (forward P/E above 22 vs a ~19 average). That is a real echo of 1999. But a steep run-up is not a timing signal: markets sat near highs for months before 2000 and 2007 turned, and 2020's crash had nothing to do with valuation at all. Past chart shapes do not predict the next path, and "we are at a peak" is only ever knowable in hindsight. This is an illustration for context, not investment advice.

      A HONEST WORD ON TIMING
      The loudest bears here have real track records: Burry, Grantham and Eisman all flagged 2008 (Grantham also 2000 and Japan). But the same record carries the warning. Burry shorted the housing market well before it cracked and sat through painful losses first, and plenty of dot-com bears were right about the bubble yet wrong on the P&L because they were a year or two early. Being early is, in money terms, the same as being wrong. Note too that today's index has already blown through most of the 2026 year-end targets the bulls set back in January, which is itself a sign of how fast sentiment has run. Nobody on this page knows the date. The signals below tend to confirm a turn only once it is underway, which is exactly why a rules-based plan (position sizing, diversification, rebalancing, an agreed reaction to specific triggers) tends to beat trying to call the top and the bottom by feel. This is educational context, not financial advice, and I am not a financial adviser.
